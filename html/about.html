About Ieoh Ming Pei

Ieoh Ming Pei (1917-2019), a major figure in 20th-century architecture, a renowned founder of a firm, and the "greatest interpreter of International style" (Treccani), emigrated from China to the United States in 1935. He received a degree from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (his thesis was awarded a Gold Medal by the American Institute of Architects) and became a U.S. citizen in 1954. He later studied under Walter Gropius at Harvard.

Pei went on to design many famous buildings, including the John F. Kennedy Presidential Library and Museum, the East Building of the National Gallery of Art in Washington D.C., the glass pyramid at the Louvre, the Museum of Islamic Art, and the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame. Pei left his mark on innovative buildings around the world with elegant geometric shapes of stone, concrete, glass, and steel, earning him countless architectural honors throughout his long and legendary career.

Life of Ieoh Ming Pei

1917 – 1935
Early Life in China

Ieoh Ming Pei was born into a prominent family in Guangzhou, China on April 26, 1917. Growing up in a magnificent home in Guangzhou called the "Lion Grove Garden", Pei was exposed to a rich cultural and intellectual environment. His family valued education, and he was sent to study in the United States at the age of 18. However, Pei never forgot his cultural heritage and often visited China throughout his life.

1935 – 1948
Early Years in the United States

In 1935, Pei emigrated to the United States and began his education in architecture. He received a degree from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) and later studied at Harvard under Walter Gropius. Pei's thesis was awarded a Gold Medal by the American Institute of Architects, and he became a US citizen in 1954.

1948 – 1960
Webb & Knapp Real Estate Corp

In 1948, Pei started working for William Zeckendorf's company, Webb & Knapp. While there, he focused on real estate and commercial design, becoming known as a "housing architect" excluded from mainstream American architecture. Although Pei completed many projects for Webb & Knapp, the quality of his architectural work was often criticized as mediocre and unremarkable. This led him to rethink his future prospects, and he eventually left the company to establish his own firm.

1958 – 1965
I.M. Pei & Associates

From 1958 to 1965, Ieoh Ming Pei worked with his partners Henry N. Cobb and Eason Leonard at their firm, I.M. Pei & Associates. During this time, Pei and his team designed several notable buildings, including the National Center for Atmospheric Research in Boulder, Colorado, which features a distinctive modernist style with angled walls and roofs that mimic the surrounding mountains. These buildings helped establish Pei's reputation as one of the leading architects of his time, paving the way for his later success with more high-profile projects.

1966 – 1989
I.M. Pei & Partners

Ieoh Ming Pei renamed his firm to I.M. Pei & Partners and completed some of his most iconic and significant works. One of his most well-known designs during this period is the East Building of the National Gallery of Art in Washington, D.C., which features a triangular shape and a dramatic, angular design that complements the neoclassical style of the adjacent West Building. Another iconic project from this period is the glass pyramid at the Louvre Museum in Paris, which serves as the museum's main entrance. The pyramid's sleek, modernist design stands in stark contrast to the Louvre's historic architecture, and has become one of the most recognizable landmarks in Paris. Pei's work during this period continued to reflect his modernist aesthetic, characterized by bold geometries, innovative use of materials, and a sensitivity to the context and history of the sites where his buildings were constructed. These projects cemented Pei's reputation as one of the most important architects of the 20th century, and helped to establish a new era of architectural design.

1990 – 2019
Retirement and Independent Practice

Ieoh Ming Pei officially retired from his firm, I.M. Pei & Partners, but continued to practice architecture independently. And during this period, he received several notable awards and honors. In 1992, he was awarded the prestigious Pritzker Architecture Prize, which is often referred to as the Nobel Prize of architecture. Pei also received the Royal Gold Medal from the Royal Institute of British Architects in 2010. Additionally, he was awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom, the highest civilian honor in the United States. Ieoh Ming Pei passed away on May 16, 2019 at the age of 102 in New York City. His innovative designs and contributions to the field of architecture have had a profound impact on the built environment and continue to inspire architects and designers around the world.
